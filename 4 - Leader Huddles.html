Caught, Not Taught

Run the Huddle
Core8 min
Paul's whole plan for the next generation fits in one verse: what you caught from me, hand to someone who can hand it to someone else. Leadership is caught, not taught. That's why we use a ladder instead of a lecture: (1) I do, you watch, we talk. (2) I do, you help, we talk. (3) You do, I help, we talk. (4) You do, I watch, we talk. (5) You do. Most of us get stuck on rung one forever — we do it all and never move students or newer leaders up. Gripping the Plow means intentional handoff, not just carrying the whole load yourself. Pick one real task — leading a discussion question, praying to close, texting a student who missed. Which rung is your student leader or newer volunteer on for that task right now, and what's the next rung? Name it.
Prayer3 min

This Week2 min

Leader Notes

Core development huddle — pairs well with recruiting or when student leaders are coming online. MSM tweak: student leaders are usually on rungs 1-2; keep tasks small and celebrate loudly. HSM tweak: high schoolers can often reach rungs 4-5 on real responsibilities — don't underestimate them.

Run the Huddle

Every group has a talker and a quiet one. Your job is to move the quiet student from Come & See toward Come & Follow — and you can't do that if the same two voices fill every silence. LGR-02

Asking Better Questions

Run the Huddle
Core8 min
Jesus already knew the blind man wanted to see. He asked anyway — 'What do you want me to do for you?' — because a good question invites a person to open up instead of just receiving. We rush to give answers; Jesus made space with questions. Here's the tool: closed questions have one right answer and end the conversation ('Was David scared?'). Open questions have no single right answer and start one ('When have you been that scared?'). Closed questions test; open questions invite. Moving a student from Come & See toward Come & Follow happens in the open ones. Practice now: take one closed question — 'Do you pray?' — and as a group, turn it into three open ones. Then do it again with a passage you're teaching this week.
Prayer3 min
Ask God to make you curious about your students — genuinely interested, not just fishing for the 'right' answer. Pray for one student you want to draw out this week.
This Week2 min
Next group, catch yourself before every closed question and open it up. Aim to ask more than you tell.
Leader Notes

Practical skills week — the turn-it-into-open-questions drill is the point, don't skip it. MSM tweak: middle schoolers need shorter, more concrete open questions ('What's the scariest thing at your school?') — abstract ones get blank stares.

The Four Chairs, Quick

Run the Huddle
Core8 min
Discipleship isn't one-size-fits-all — students are in different chairs, and each chair needs a different move. Quick version: Chair 1, Come & See — lost or exploring, not sure about Jesus. Chair 2, Come & Follow — a believer, following Jesus. Chair 3, Be With Me — equipped and starting to serve. Chair 4, Remain In Me — a disciple-maker, pouring into others. 1 John says the goal is to 'live as Jesus did' — Jesus met people right where they sat and moved them one chair at a time. He didn't hand a Chair 1 student a leadership role, and he didn't leave a Chair 3 student sitting on their hands. Now do the work: have each leader take their roster and quickly mark a chair number next to each student. Then pick two — one earlier, one further along — and name the single next-step action for each. That's the diagnosis; the action is the win.

Leader Notes

02 · Leading Groups25
LGR-04

When the Conversation Goes Sideways

Run the Huddle
Core8 min
It'll happen: a student drops a controversial take, derails onto something wild, or says something heavy that stops the room. Your instinct is to shut it down or win the argument. Paul says lead with grace — 'gracious and attractive' — so you have the right response for everyone. Three moves. (1) Off-topic: honor it, then steer. 'Good rabbit trail — let's come back to it after, I want to hear the rest.' (2) Controversial: get curious before you correct. 'Tell me more about why you see it that way' buys you truth and trust. (3) Heavy — abuse, self-harm, real pain: don't fix it in the circle. Slow down, stay calm, and take it 1-on-1, then loop in your Team Leader or staff. You don't have to have the answer; you have to be safe. Practice one: someone plays a student with a spicy take, and a leader practices getting curious instead of correcting.
Prayer3 min
Ask God for gracious, steady hearts — and for the wisdom to know when to steer, when to get curious, and when to slow down and go 1-on-1. Pray for any student you already know is carrying something heavy.
This Week2 min
This week, if the conversation goes sideways, lead with one question instead of one correction. And know your campus's next step for the heavy stuff before you need it.
Leader Notes

Important safety/skills huddle. Make sure every leader knows the campus escalation path for disclosures (abuse, self-harm) — this is not optional and not peer-led guesswork. HSM tweak: high schoolers bring heavier and more controversial topics — spend more time on moves 2 and 3. MSM tweak: middle schoolers derail more with silliness — spend more time on move 1.

LGR-05

Silence Is Your Friend

Run the Huddle
Core8 min
There's 'a time to be quiet.' Most of us can't stand it. You ask a question, nobody answers, and after two seconds you panic and rescue it — you answer your own question, or reword it three times, or move on. Here's the truth: the student who was about to speak just got let off the hook. The silence wasn't dead air; it was a kid working up the nerve, or actually thinking. When you rescue silence, you train your group that they never have to answer — you'll always fill it. So don't. Ask the question once. Then wait. Count to ten in your head if you have to. The pause belongs to them. Practice now: someone asks a real question, and the whole group sits in ten full seconds of silence. Feel how long that actually is — that's the point.
Prayer3 min
Ask God for the self-control to stay quiet and trust the pause. Pray for the specific students who only speak when you make space for them.
This Week2 min
Next group, ask your question once and then count to ten before you say another word. Let the silence do its job at least twice.
Leader Notes

Short, high-impact skills huddle — the ten-second silence drill is uncomfortable and completely necessary, don't cut it. MSM tweak: middle schoolers need slightly shorter waits and more permission ('there's no wrong answer here') before they'll fill the pause.

LGR-06

Preparing to Lead a Group

Run the Huddle
Core8 min
You don't need a seminary degree to prep — you need a rhythm. Paul says Scripture itself equips you for the good work; your job is just to show up ready to let it. Here's the whole prep in four steps, about fifteen minutes: OPEN — read the passage and the questions once, early in the week, not in the car. READ — read it again slower and ask 'what's this actually about?' so you're not surprised. DISCUSS — pick the two or three questions that matter most and think about how your specific students might answer. PRAY — pray through your roster before you ever walk in. That's it. Prep isn't about having every answer; it's about not being caught flat-footed so you can actually be present with students. Ask the group: which of those four do you skip most, and what would it take to build the fifteen-minute rhythm into your week?
Prayer3 min
Ask God to make His Word come alive in your prep this week — and to prepare your heart before you ever prepare your talking points. Pray for the discipline to build the rhythm.

Leader Notes

Foundational skills huddle — great for new leaders or a team that's been winging it. Consider handing out the Open/Read/Discuss/Pray steps on a card. MSM tweak: middle school prep leans heavier on 'how will MY students actually answer this' — the questions land differently at 12 than 17.
